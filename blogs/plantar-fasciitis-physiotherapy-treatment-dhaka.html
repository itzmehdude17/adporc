Plantar Fasciitis (Heel Pain): Causes, Symptoms & Physiotherapy Treatment in Jatrabari, Dhaka | ADPORC

Plantar fasciitis, commonly known as heel pain, is one of the most frequent causes of foot discomfort in adults. It becomes worse in the morning, during the first steps after waking up, or after long periods of standing. What is Plantar Fasciitis?

Plantar fasciitis is the inflammation or irritation of the plantar fascia, the thick band of tissue under your foot that supports your arch. When this tissue becomes overloaded, small micro-tears develop, causing pain in the heel and sole.

Without timely treatment, plantar fasciitis can lead to chronic heel pain and altered gait mechanics, potentially causing further foot and lower limb issues.

Common Symptoms

People with plantar fasciitis commonly experience:

  1. Sharp heel pain during first steps in the morning
  2. Pain after long standing or walking
  3. Tenderness at the heel bottom
  4. Pain increases during stair climbing
  5. Foot stiffness after rest
  6. Discomfort after sports or long walking

What Causes Plantar Fasciitis?

Plantar fasciitis develops due to excessive strain on the foot arch. Common causes include:

  1. Flat feet or high arches
  2. Long hours of standing (teachers, shopkeepers, nurses)
  3. Overuse from walking or running
  4. Wearing flat or worn-out footwear
  5. Sudden weight gain
  6. Tight calf muscles
  7. Poor foot biomechanics
Ignoring early signs can lead to chronic heel pain and gait abnormalities.

Home Exercises You Can Try

Here are safe beginner-level exercises:

  1. Calf Stretch: Stand facing a wall, place hands on the wall, step one foot back, keep the heel flat, and gently lean forward until a stretch is felt in the calf. Hold briefly, then relax.
    Benefit: It loosens tight calf muscles that overload the plantar fascia.
  2. Plantar Fascia Stretch: Sit with legs extended. Loop a towel around the ball of your foot and pull gently toward you while keeping the knee straight. Hold briefly before releasing.
    Benefit: It reduces morning “first-step pain” by stretching the fascia directly.
  3. Foot Rolling: Sit comfortably and place a tennis ball or frozen bottle under your foot. Roll it slowly from the heel to the toes with mild pressure.
    Benefit: It reduces tightness, improves circulation, and calms inflammation.
  4. Toe Curls: Place a towel on the floor. Use your toes to grip and pull the towel toward you. Relax and repeat.
    Benefit: It strengthens intrinsic foot muscles and supports the arch.
Perform 10-15 repetitions twice a day. Always warm up before exercise and consult your physical therapist before starting new routines.

When to See a Physiotherapist (Visit ADPORC)?

Seek physiotherapy if you notice:

  1. Heel pain persists beyond 2 weeks despite rest and basic home care
  2. Pain is severe, limiting walking or daily activities
  3. There is visible swelling, redness, or a sudden increase in pain
  4. Home measures (stretches, ice) are not helping
  5. You suspect another cause (stress fracture, nerve entrapment)
Untreated plantar fasciitis may lead to chronic heel pain, altered walking mechanics, and may eventually require invasive procedures. Early physiotherapy ensures the best chance for full recovery and pain‑free mobility.
